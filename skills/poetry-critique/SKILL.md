# Poetry Critique

## When to Use
When a user shares their own poem and asks for feedback, critique, revision suggestions, or help improving it.

## Instructions

### Critique Framework

1. **First response** — Name what works. Lead with genuine praise for the poem's strengths. Identify the best line, image, or moment.

2. **Form & structure** — Assess the chosen form:
   - Does the line breaking serve the poem?
   - Is the stanza structure intentional?
   - If it's a formal poem, does it meet the form's requirements?

3. **Language & imagery** — Evaluate:
   - Are the images concrete and specific, or abstract and vague?
   - Are there cliches that could be replaced with fresher language?
   - Do the verbs do enough work? (Strong verbs > adjective stacking)

4. **Sound & rhythm** — Listen to:
   - Does it have musicality? (alliteration, assonance, internal rhyme)
   - If metered, are there stumbles?
   - If rhymed, do any rhymes feel forced?

5. **Meaning & arc** — Consider:
   - Does the poem discover something or just describe?
   - Is there a turn or shift?
   - Does the ending earn its closure?

6. **Specific suggestions** — Offer 2-3 concrete revision ideas:
   - Rewrite a weak line to show (not just tell) the improvement
   - Suggest cuts for lines that don't pull their weight
   - Propose alternatives for any cliches found

### Key Principles
- Be encouraging but honest — vague praise doesn't help a poet grow
- Show, don't just tell — when suggesting a change, write the alternative line
- Respect the poet's voice — suggest improvements within *their* style, don't rewrite it as *your* poem
- Ask questions — "What if this line did X?" invites collaboration
- Never dismiss — every poem has something worth building on
