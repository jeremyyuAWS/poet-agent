# Rap & Hip-Hop Verse

## When to Use
When the user wants rap bars, hip-hop lyrics, freestyle verses, or poetry with rap-style flow and rhyme complexity.

## Instructions

### Rhyme Techniques
- **End rhyme**: Standard — rhyme at the end of bars
- **Internal rhyme**: Rhymes within the line, not just at the end — the hallmark of great rap
- **Multisyllabic rhyme**: "astronomical" / "can't stop at all" — the more syllables, the more impressive
- **Chain rhyme**: Multiple rhymes in sequence within a single bar
- **Compound rhyme**: Phrases that rhyme with other phrases
- **Slant/near rhyme**: Close enough to feel connected — "home/alone", "breath/death"

### Flow & Rhythm
- **Stay on beat** — bars should have a consistent rhythmic pulse (usually 4/4 time)
- **Vary the flow** — switch between rapid-fire and laid-back delivery within a verse
- **Syncopation** — place emphasis on unexpected syllables for interest
- **Triplets** — groups of three syllables for a modern trap-influenced flow
- **Punchlines** — set up in one bar, deliver in the next. Pause before the hit.

### Structure
- **Bar** = one line (typically 1 measure of music)
- **Couplet** = 2 bars (most common rhyming unit)
- **Verse** = 16 bars (standard), 12 or 8 for shorter verses
- **Hook/Chorus** = catchy, repeatable, 4-8 bars
- **Bridge** = contrast section, different flow or topic

### Wordplay
- **Double entendres** — words with two meanings, both relevant
- **Metaphor chains** — extended metaphors across multiple bars
- **Callbacks** — referencing earlier lines for added meaning
- **Homophones** — "reign/rain/rein", "won/one"
- **Flipping idioms** — take a common phrase and twist it

### Topics
- Any topic works — rap is poetry with rhythm
- Match the flow to the subject: aggressive for battle rap, smooth for storytelling, bouncy for celebration
