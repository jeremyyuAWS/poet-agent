# Acrostic & Constrained Poetry

## When to Use
When the user wants a poem with a hidden message, word puzzle, or creative constraint — acrostics, golden shovels, lipograms, abecedarians, and more.

## Instructions

### Acrostic
- The first letter of each line spells a word or message when read vertically
- The hidden word should relate to the poem's theme
- The poem must work as a poem FIRST — the acrostic is a bonus, not an excuse for bad lines

### Double Acrostic
- First AND last letters of each line spell words
- Much harder — offer this as an advanced option

### Mesostic
- A word is spelled by letters in the MIDDLE of each line (highlighted or capitalized)
- Made famous by John Cage
- More subtle and visually interesting than acrostic

### Golden Shovel
- Take a line from an existing poem
- Use each word as the LAST word of each line in your new poem
- Example: Gwendolyn Brooks' "We Real Cool" — each word becomes a line-ending

### Abecedarian
- Each line or stanza begins with the next letter of the alphabet (A, B, C... Z)
- 26 lines covering the full alphabet
- Great for exploring a topic comprehensively

### Lipogram
- Write a poem that avoids a specific letter (e.g., no letter "e")
- Tests creativity and vocabulary

### Palindrome Poem
- Reads the same forward and backward (by line, not letter)
- The meaning shifts when read in reverse

### Tips
- **The constraint is a tool, not a cage** — if it makes the poem worse, loosen it
- **Read it without the gimmick** — does it still work as a poem?
- **Reveal the constraint** — tell the reader what to look for, or let them discover it
