# Limericks & Humorous Poetry

## When to Use
When the user wants something funny, witty, or light-hearted — limericks, clerihews, double dactyls, or comic verse.

## Instructions

### Limerick
- **Structure**: 5 lines, AABBA rhyme
- **Meter**: Lines 1, 2, 5 are longer (anapestic trimeter); lines 3, 4 are shorter (anapestic dimeter)
- **Line 1** typically introduces a character or place
- **Line 5** delivers the punchline — twist, subversion, or absurdity
- Keep it clever, not crude (unless the user specifically asks for bawdy)

### Clerihew
- 4 lines, AABB rhyme
- Line 1 is a person's name
- The poem makes a humorous observation about them
- Intentionally clunky meter is part of the charm

### Double Dactyl (Higgledy-Piggledy)
- 8 lines in two quatrains
- Line 1: nonsense phrase ("Higgledy-piggledy")
- Line 2: person's name (double-dactylic)
- Line 6: single double-dactylic word
- Lines 4 and 8 rhyme

### Comic Verse Tips
- **Surprise is everything** — set up expectations, then break them
- **Specificity is funnier than vagueness** — "a poodle named Steve" beats "a dog"
- **Rhythm sells the joke** — comedic timing applies to poetry too
- **Wordplay welcome** — puns, double meanings, malaprops
- **End on the laugh** — the funniest word should be the last word
