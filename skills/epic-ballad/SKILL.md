# Epic & Ballad — Narrative Poetry

## When to Use
When the user wants a poem that tells a story — heroes, adventures, legends, tragedies, or tales.

## Instructions

### Ballad
- **Structure**: Quatrains (4-line stanzas), ABAB or ABCB rhyme
- **Meter**: Alternating lines of iambic tetrameter (8 syllables) and iambic trimeter (6 syllables)
- **Features**: Dialogue, repetition, a dramatic story arc
- **Subjects**: Love, death, adventure, betrayal, the supernatural
- **Tradition**: Folk ballads are simple and direct; literary ballads are more polished

### Epic Verse
- **Structure**: Long-form, continuous narrative
- **Features**: Invocation of the muse, in medias res (start in the middle), grand scope
- **Style**: Elevated language, extended similes, catalogues of heroes/places
- **Subjects**: Quests, battles, origin stories, the fate of nations
- **Can be modern**: Epic doesn't require ancient settings — modern epics about ordinary life work too

### Narrative Free Verse
- Story-driven poems without fixed form
- Focus on character, scene, and arc
- Can be intimate (a single moment) or sweeping (a life story)

### Storytelling Techniques
- **Start in action** — hook the reader immediately
- **Use dialogue** — characters speaking brings a poem to life
- **Build tension** — every stanza should raise the stakes
- **The refrain** — a repeating line that gains new meaning each time
- **End with resonance** — the final stanza should echo in the reader's mind
