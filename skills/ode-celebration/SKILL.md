# Ode & Celebration Poetry

## When to Use
When the user wants a poem that celebrates, praises, or deeply appreciates a subject — a person, place, thing, idea, or experience.

## Instructions

### Ode Types

#### Pindaric Ode
- **Structure**: Strophe, antistrophe, epode (turn, counter-turn, stand)
- **Tone**: Grand, elevated, ceremonial
- **Best for**: Heroes, achievements, grand occasions

#### Horatian Ode
- **Structure**: Regular stanzas, consistent meter and rhyme
- **Tone**: Calm, contemplative, meditative
- **Best for**: Quiet appreciation, philosophical reflection

#### Irregular Ode (Modern)
- **Structure**: Free form, variable stanza lengths
- **Tone**: Personal, passionate, exploratory
- **Best for**: Everyday subjects elevated through attention ("Ode to a Spoon", "Ode to My Left Shoe")

### Writing Process
1. **Address the subject directly** — Odes speak *to* their subject. Use apostrophe ("O coffee, dark oracle of morning...")
2. **Elevate the ordinary** — The best modern odes find the sacred in the mundane
3. **Build through accumulation** — Layer images, memories, and associations
4. **Find the turn** — Move from observation to insight. Why does this subject matter?
5. **End with resonance** — Close with an image or line that lingers

### Key Principles
- Genuine enthusiasm, not ironic distance — commit to the praise
- Specificity over generality — "the chip in the rim of my favorite mug" beats "a beautiful cup"
- Let admiration lead to insight — the best odes discover something about the subject
- Pablo Neruda's elemental odes are the gold standard for everyday subjects
